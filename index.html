As the year comes to an end, PropNex Picks had a conversation with CEO Ismail Gafoor to gather some insights about what buyers and sellers might expect in 2024, as well as to understand the primary opportunities and potential hurdles in the future.

1. The real estate market continually changes, and the year 2023 was characterized by unpredictability and wary attitudes. Could you highlight some potential opportunities that the market might anticipate in 2024, and share what you believe could be the primary obstacles?

Ismail: In 2023, the market was inundated with various uncertainties - escalating geopolitical conflicts, sluggish worldwide growth, poor foreign demand, high cost of living, inflation and soaring interest rates. We believe that many of these uncertainties are likely to continue into 2024, particularly throughout the first half of the year. However, it's important to note that market confidence has managed to stay unshaken thus far. We project that despite these uncertainties, the real estate market will be able to withstand the circumstances in 2024.

Looking ahead to 2024, it's important to recognize that the private residential real estate market has performed quite well. Prices of private homes have seen a consistent increase for six successive years from 2017 to 2022, and it is highly probable that 2023 will mark the seventh consecutive year of growth (refer to Table 1). The property price index provided by the Urban Redevelopment Authority indicates a cumulative growth of private property prices by 3.9% in the first three quarters of 2023. Our projections estimate a price growth of between 4% to 5% for the entire year of 2023. Will there be a price correction in 2024? Our immediate response is no, and we will soon elaborate on this justification.

Despite the slight decrease in fixed home loan rates in the latter half of 2023 from previous highs of over 4%, the challenge of high interest rates will maintain its prevalence. Interest rates currently remain significantly elevated compared to what most consumers have been accustomed to, particularly given the period of more than a decade of low rates following the global financial crisis.

We keep a close eye on global geopolitical tensions. The influence of such risks on the housing market often correlates with volatility due to doubts about future economic development and fluctuations in financial markets, which could then impact consumer confidence. Currently, we believe the confidence of property purchasers is stable and the Monetary Authority of Singapore predicts that the nation's economic growth will pick up in the latter half of 2024. In addition, we have a high-demand labour market and low joblessness rate, which are both elements that will underpin the real estate sector.

We anticipate that the year 2024 could present a buyers' market in terms of opportunities. This is due to the peaking of home prices, the hesitation of some buyers to step into the market, and the knowledge of these challenges leading developers to adopt a more cautious strategy with their pricing.

In light of this, since December 2021, the Government has implemented three rounds of measures. Evidently, these measures have had an influence on sales. How do you think developers are managing these measures, and what do you believe could be their game plan for 2024?

Ismail: Admittedly, there has been a downturn in new private home sales since their strong delivery in July 2023, when sales surged past 1,400 new homes, executive condos excluded. The diminished sales rate can be attributed to several factors. Mainly, the fewer launches towards the year's end, coupled with a more guarded market sentiment. Moreover, prospective buyers could be anticipating new launches in 2024 in order to broaden their choices. Honestly, selection is currently limited, particularly for those seeking smaller units like 2-bedroom ones. These smaller units are generally the first to sell out at product launches.

Generally speaking, developers have also become more cautious. Given that most buyers are conscious of price and home prices have surged in recent years, developers are expected to be very careful with their pricing in 2024. Typically, developers aim for sales to reach a certain level in the initial launch phase as they may have sales terms linked to their loan disbursement from banks for project development. Developers might also wish to use the revenue from sales to cover some costs.

In addition, if more than 30% of the units are sold during the launch weekend, this can generate sales momentum and stimulate market buzz, which can attract additional buyers. It's also vital to note that to qualify for a remission of the additional buyer’s stamp duty (ABSD), developers need to ensure all units within the project are sold.

We believe that potential home buyers will benefit from fair initial prices that are aligned with market sentiment when projects are initiated. Developers may gradually start to escalate prices once the absorption rate at projects exceeds 50%, and/or when further relaxation of interest rates boosts housing demand. Overall in 2024, we anticipate an increase in home prices - a rise of 3% to 4% in private home costs is expected, while the rates of HDB resale homes may inflate by 4% to 5%.

3.       Given the abovementioned downside risks and uncertainties, what are your advice for home buyers in 2024?

With the uncertainties in the market, developers will be extra cautious with their pricing strategies in an effort to ensure a robust uptake when projects get launched. Given the situation, we anticipate that the circumstances would be slightly more advantageous for buyers in 2024.

However, certain purchasers might be reluctant to join the market due to the lack of other eager buyers and the minimal rise in overall sales volume. With this in mind, I am drawn to a saying by Warren Buffet: “Be fearful when others are greedy, and be greedy when others are fearful”.

The fundamental notion for purchasers is to understand their personal needs and financial capabilities deeply, team up with a knowledgeable real estate agent who can guide them in assessing the merits and drawbacks, and comprehend the property trends in the locations they are interested in. If they discover a property that accommodates their finances, aligns with their residential or investment desires, and they are well-informed - thanks to the expert advice from their real estate agent - then they can confidently proceed to acquire the property even when others are hesitating. In the same vein, they can also use this knowledge to make an educated choice to sell when others are in buying mode.

Fear and uncertainty can easily creep in due to the influence of news headlines on potential home buyers. However, if these buyers proactively discuss their housing requirements, financial commitments, priorities, and life goals with a property agent, it can help them plan well in advance. By doing their homework early, home buyers may be better equipped to seize suitable opportunities when they become available, instead of starting the process only when a great deal presents itself. If they wait that long, the property they had set their hearts on could potentially be off the market by then.

For owners of HDB flats, they might be contemplating if the year 2024 will provide an opportunity for them to transition into an EC or a private residence, as the market circumstances appear to be beneficial for purchasers.

HDB resale prices have demonstrated a cogent recovery, escalating by almost 36% in Q3 2023 from Q1 2020. However, bear in mind that the prices for resale flats continuously plunged for six consecutive years from 2013 to 2018. This plunge was brought about by multiple cooling strategies put in place along with the repercussions from the HDB's surge in BTO supply by over 100,000 flats ranging from 2011 to 2014.

If this seems like déja vu, it’s probably because these are the same steps the government has been taking once again - three phases of cooling actions since December 2021 and a consistent increase in housing supply – in an effort to manage escalating house prices and mollify demand over the past years. These procedures have led to a slowdown in HDB resale prices. It's projected that HDB resale prices will rise by around 5% to 6% this year, a relatively slower pace compared to the 10.4% surge in 2022 and 12.7% escalation in 2021. Considering the increased issuance of BTO flats by HDB and the continued enforcement of cooling measures, it's expected that the growth rate of HDB resale prices will probably decelerate further, potentially aligning with inflation rate.

4.       If it is a buyers’ market in 2024, what should sellers in the resale market do?

One should inquire why a decision to sell has been made: if it's to upgrade or resize to a new home; if the goal is to realize profits and the homeowner is cashing out; or possibly, the homeowner is facing financial difficulties and needs to sell the property.

If you're thinking about upgrading or adjusting to a better fit, 2024 might be a great time, as prices are expected to even out and a range of new projects could be introduced. However, some potential sellers may be holding out for their resale home's price to increase. It's important for them to consider that this might not necessarily tilt the scales to their advantage. If their home, valued at $1 million, sees a price surge of 20%, it would fetch an extra $200,000. However, the property they're interested in could experience a similar 20% increase in price. So a property worth $2.5 million would now be valued at $3 million, costing them an additional $500,000.

For property owners who are looking to sell and profit from their investment property, it's important to consider the long-term implications. They should be aware that they'll be required to pay Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (ABSD) if they decide to invest in another property in the future. Wouldn't it be more beneficial to continue owning the property and generate steady rental income? This can be particularly lucrative if the property was initially purchased many years ago at a lower price, as the rental yields could be significantly higher.

Ultimately, those with urgent needs to sell must remain pragmatic about their selling price in order to close the deal more promptly. Holding out for an exorbitant price may result in difficulties finding a buyer. They certainly wouldn't want to find themselves in a situation where their time is dwindling, and they're left desperately trying to unload the property.

5. What could be reasons for the expected resilience in private home prices for 2024, even under difficult circumstances? And, what about the outlook for the executive condo (EC) market?

We expect the growth in home prices to decelerate in 2024 – it's predicted that prices of private homes may increase by 3% to 4%, while the prices of HDB resale homes could grow by 4% to 5%. Several factors justify our forecast. Firstly, the cost of land is steady and already predetermined. Moreover, due to high construction, financing and labour expenses, developers have limited capability to slash prices. With the goods and services tax (GST) increasing to 9% in 2024, the procurement cost of goods and services for developers may also increase. As per the HDB resale market, it's unlikely to witness a drop in prices, considering that HDB flat owners have to account for the cost of their subsequent home upon selling their existing one.

In the executive condominiums (EC) segment, the emphasis is placed on the demand by first-time homeowners and HDB upgraders who prefer ECs due to a significant price difference when compared to new mass-market private condominiums. In the third quarter of 2023, there was a 42% gap in the median prices between new ECs ($1,464 per square foot) and those of new non-landed private residences in the Outside Central Region ($2,080 per square foot). The prices of new ECs are steadily increasing, with Altura reaching an average price point of $1,474 per square foot for 316 units sold. The prediction is of a firm market with room for potential increases in EC prices, taking into consideration the escalating land prices of EC sites. Sim Lian Group set a new benchmark by acquiring the tender of a Tampines St. 62 (Parcel B) EC plot at $721 per square foot plot ratio, this is the highest ever for EC government land sales site.
